History

The modern history of rafting on the Colorado River began with John Wesley Powell, a geologist and one-armed Civil War veteran. In 1869, Powell led a daring expedition down the river through the Grand Canyon, a feat no one had attempted before. Using wooden boats and limited knowledge of the river's terrain, Powell and his crew encountered towering canyon walls, treacherous rapids, and unknown dangers. Despite losing boats and supplies, they completed the journey, documenting the geography and character of the river. Powell's expedition opened the eyes of the world to the Colorado River's beauty and challenges.

The turning point came in the mid-20th century, following World War II. The invention and military use of durable rubber rafts during the war made rafting safer and more accessible. After the war, surplus rafts were repurposed by adventurers who sought to conquer the river's rapids. By the 1950s, white water rafting on the Colorado River had transformed into a recreational activity. Entrepreneurs established rafting companies to guide thrill-seekers through the river's wild waters, especially in the Grand Canyon. These tours became increasingly popular as more people sought outdoor adventures.
